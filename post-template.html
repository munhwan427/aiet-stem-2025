Essay December 2025

AI Evolution and Its Impact on Educational Assessment

NS
Namsoo Shin
Michigan State University · CREATE for STEM Institute

A focused breakdown of AI's progression from expert systems to generative models, and what this transformation means for how we assess student learning.

The Three Waves of AI in Education

When we look at the history of artificial intelligence in educational settings, we can identify three distinct waves, each bringing its own affordances and limitations for assessment.

Wave 1: Expert Systems (1980s-2000s)

The first wave relied on carefully crafted rules and decision trees. Systems like MYCIN in medicine or early intelligent tutoring systems encoded expert knowledge into if-then rules. For assessment, this meant:

  • Highly predictable responses based on predefined patterns
  • Limited to domains where expertise could be fully articulated
  • Assessment focused on matching student responses to expected answers

Wave 2: Machine Learning (2000s-2010s)

The second wave introduced statistical pattern recognition. Systems learned from data rather than explicit rules. This enabled:

  • Automated essay scoring based on feature extraction
  • Learning analytics that could identify at-risk students
  • Personalized learning paths based on performance patterns

Wave 3: Generative AI (2020s-Present)

The current wave fundamentally changes the game. Large language models can generate human-like responses, engage in dialogue, and adapt to context in ways previous systems could not.

"AI won't replace humans. But humans who don't use AI will be replaced by humans who do."

Implications for Assessment

This evolution forces us to rethink what we're actually trying to assess. If a student can use AI to produce a well-written essay, what are we really measuring? Here are key considerations:

Key Takeaways

  • Process over product: We need to assess how students engage with AI tools, not just their final outputs.
  • Critical evaluation: Can students identify when AI-generated content is incorrect or misleading?
  • Prompt engineering: The quality of the question determines the quality of the answer.
  • Domain knowledge matters: AI tools amplify existing knowledge rather than replacing the need for it.

A Path Forward

Rather than viewing AI as a threat to traditional assessment, we should see it as an opportunity to assess higher-order skills that have always been important but were difficult to measure:

  1. Metacognition: How well do students understand their own learning process?
  2. Information synthesis: Can students combine multiple sources (including AI) into coherent understanding?
  3. Ethical reasoning: Do students understand when and how to use AI appropriately?

The transition won't be easy, but it's necessary. Our assessment systems must evolve as quickly as the technologies our students will use in their careers.
